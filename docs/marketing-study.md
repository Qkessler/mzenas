## Buyer personas

Mzenas serves two clearly different users: the operator, who pays, and the diner, who generates the data and the network effect.

### The independent operator

Carlos is 38 to 55 years old and runs a bar or restaurant with 5 to 30 tables in a mid-sized or large Spanish city. His digital skills are basic — WhatsApp and not much else. He employs between 2 and 5 people and often serves tables himself. Annual revenue sits between €60,000 and €250,000.

His frustrations are concrete: he can't find or afford waitstaff, order errors cost him returns and bad moods, every system he's seen was complicated to set up, he has no time to train on new tools, and he's tried apps that nobody ended up using. His motivations are equally concrete: cut costs without cutting quality, keep the business running smoothly at peak, turn tables faster, and have customers leave satisfied without waiting.

His key phrase: *"If you tell me I'll be taking orders in ten minutes, I'll believe it when I see it."*

He reaches Mzenas through recommendations from another operator he trusts, a commercial visit at his own venue, a Google search for "restaurant order software without POS," or seeing the product in use at a nearby bar.

### The urban diner

Laura is 25 to 42 years old, a working professional with mid-to-high income, and she eats out two to four times a week. Her tech comfort is high — she uses delivery, reservation, and streaming apps every day. She wants experiences that are fast, autonomous, and frictionless.

Her frustrations: waiting for a waiter to order or pay, generic recommendations that ignore her tastes, no visibility into what's on offer right now, and trendy restaurants that disappoint. Her motivations: order and pay from her phone without installing anything, discover new places that fit what she likes, see exclusive real-time offers (Hot Moments), and get recommendations based on her actual history.

Her key phrase: *"I hate waiting for the waiter to order. If I can do it from my phone, so much the better."*

## PESTEL analysis

The macro environment for Mzenas is structurally favourable. Tailwinds — digitisation, tourism, AI, public subsidies — clearly outweigh regulatory friction.

### Political

The Hospitality Digitisation Plan (Kit Digital) funds up to €6,000 per SME for management software. Mzenas can position itself as an eligible solution. The mandatory electronic invoice (Ley Crea y Crece) will force accelerated digitisation across the sector. The EU's digital payments regulatory framework is stable and favourable.

### Economic

Spanish hospitality represented 6.2% of GDP in 2024 — a structural sector, not a cyclical one. Margin pressure from rising labour costs (minimum wage increases) strengthens the incentive to digitise. International tourism keeps breaking records, bringing in more customers with advanced digital habits. Independent restaurants have little cash on hand, which makes Mzenas's €30/month entry price critical.

### Social

Millennial and Gen Z users expect digital interactions in any consumption context. Ordering food by phone (delivery) has normalised the idea of ordering without talking to anyone. Demand for personalised experiences keeps rising against generic offerings. Foreign tourists often don't speak Spanish, which makes a multilingual digital menu an immediate advantage.

### Technological

Smartphone penetration in Spain exceeds 93%, making QR-without-app universal infrastructure. Generative AI (GPT-4, Claude) enables instant onboarding from any menu format. Instant payment systems (Bizum, Apple Pay) remove friction at checkout. Cloud computing makes a hardware-free SaaS possible at near-zero marginal cost.

### Ecological

Removing paper (tickets, kitchen slips) aligns with corporate sustainability policies. Environmental impact is increasingly part of B2B tool selection.

### Legal

GDPR requires careful handling of user consumption history. Mandatory allergen display in menus (EU Regulation 1169/2011) is a requirement Mzenas resolves automatically through AI. POS and invoicing regulation (VeriFactu, TicketBAI in the Basque Country and Navarre) is a moving variable to watch.

## Go-to-market strategy

### Phase 1 — Local validation (Cartagena, 0–3 months)

The goal is 3 to 5 restaurants using the product in real service. The main channel is on-site visits to the 20 bars already identified in our contact list. We enter through close contacts first and cold visits after. The offer is a free two-week pilot with assisted configuration on the spot, backed by a one-page handout and a live demo on the operator's own phone. Success metric: first paying customer.

### Phase 2 — City growth (3–9 months)

The goal is 50 to 100 restaurants in Cartagena and Murcia. The main channel becomes operator word of mouth, supported by a part-time commercial hire. Digital activation means a LinkedIn and Instagram presence built on real, timestamped success cases. Local SEO targets searches like "restaurant order software Cartagena/Murcia." We approach the regional hospitality associations and help operators process their Kit Digital subsidies as a competitive edge. Success metric: sustainable monthly recurring revenue.

### Phase 3 — National scale (9–24 months)

The goal is presence in five or more cities and the foundation for the discovery platform. The channel mix adds a full commercial team and partnerships with hospitality sector distributors. Inbound content — a blog and SEO around hospitality digitisation — carries the long tail. We expand into tourist cities (Valencia, Málaga, Seville, Barcelona) where digital-tourist pressure is highest. Once restaurant density reaches critical mass, we activate the discovery platform. Success metric: live network effect.
